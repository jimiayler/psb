Author's Note

Earlier this century, I composed a piece for my band The Permaculture Quintet titled "Post-Singularity Blues". At that time, there was no escaping the totalizing narrative of the Singularity and all it implied and/or longed for. Among the more notable aspects of this commodified prophetic sensibility was the comfort some speculators felt with a supremacist presumption of "post-humanity" obtaining from the ostensible fusing of our being with the merely mechanical.

It does seem as though the irrational exuberance around the Singularity has cooled a little bit since then, perhaps a tacit admission that the limits of "actually-existing humanity" -- readily demonstrated this century alone by wars of choice, financial downturns and manifold incapacities in the face of forces beyond our control -- might temper the speculative frenzy surrounding technology, subcutaneous and otherwise, and its actual potential for liberation.

We are not cyborgs; we have been cyborgs since the down of tools; we've never been closer to the event horizon of this consummation devoutly to be wished -- all these statements are true and not a single one of them interests me in any way. I anticipate most if not all of the most fervent dreams of the post-humanist camp are not only possible but likely -- and what of it? Developing and releasing new advances in technology is now the bio-imperative of the greatest force alive today: capitalism, whose resilience and adaptability give the lie to its much-bruited "late" status, and many "post" statuses besides.

I conceived of this "blues" as a hybrid artwork for mobile devices exclusively, endeavoring to directly engage the inarguably transformative, boundary-blurring encroachment of that which I sought to critique, utilizing stories, computer-processed music and video and the single least-believable cyborg I could imagine to map out this complex condition, a multimedia holler for the less-than-pre-human.

    -- James Keepnews
